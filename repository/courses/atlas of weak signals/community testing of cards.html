02 — The Game

Session Walkthrough

The game was run as a video call with three colleagues from a digital transformation agency — people who use AI as a professional tool every day. They came in blind, with no prior knowledge of the game or the cards. The format was explained on the spot, making the session a genuine test of both card clarity and rule comprehension.

The prototype used a digital version of the cards (HTML game interface), allowing for real-time drawing and display. The session ran ~26 minutes including ideation.

01
Rules explanation — 2 min
Armin introduced the game structure: 4 rounds of card drawing (weak signals, opportunities, random triggers, challenge), each followed by discussion time. The methodology was framed as "a tarot card for future design" — open source, speculative, non-prescriptive.
💬
Note: The digital format required reading cards aloud. This worked but slowed the pace — a screen-share of drawn cards would improve flow.
02
2 Weak Signals drawn — 5 min
Two signals were drawn from the shuffled deck. The group immediately recognised them as present-day realities, not abstractions. The signals triggered recognition and a self-referential question: "should I ask AI about this and waste litres of water for this prompt?"
⟶ Kwh/Prompt ⟶ Public Education System for Adults
03
Opportunity cards drawn — 3 min
Three opportunity cards were drawn from the deck to frame the design space. Armin explained each verbally and encouraged participants to begin thinking about how they relate to the signals already on the table.
⟶ Screensavers ⟶ Restorative Practices ⟶ DIY Tools
04
Wild / Random Trigger cards — 3 min
Two trigger cards were drawn. "Slavery" caused an immediate strong reaction ("What the heck?"). Armin explained the card's framing around labour and coercion in the AI context, noting he'd explain later how he arrived at these cards. Both cards were described as "random" provocations to push ideas beyond the rational.
⟶ Creative Dissent ⟶ Slavery
05
Challenge card drawn — 2 min
The single challenge card set the context for all ideation. Participants were asked to use at least one card from each category in their ideas, combining them freely toward this overarching theme.
⟶ Open-Source Sufficiency
06
Ideation & sharing — 12 min
Participants were given free rein — ideas could be anything: product, policy, art, scenario. "It's called speculative design," Armin reminded when asked if ideas needed to be feasible. Each participant shared their ideas, then the group discussed and built on them together.

04 — Reflection

Key Learnings

01
Card Design
Cards that are "too real" create immediate resonance
The weak signals weren't abstract futures — participants recognised them as today's realities. This created instant engagement, but also risked collapsing speculation into current events commentary. The sweet spot is signals that are just outside normal awareness.
02
Community Context
Professional AI users go further, faster
Colleagues who work with AI daily brought domain knowledge that accelerated ideation — Kamila's Power Apps example was lived experience, not speculation. The community context shaped what ideas were possible and how concrete they got.
03
Prototype
The digital prototype worked, but showed its limits
The HTML card game enabled remote play successfully. However, reading cards aloud in a video call slowed the pace. Screen-sharing drawn cards, or a shared live view for participants, would make the game feel more simultaneous and less lecture-like.
04
Game Mechanics
The "at least one from each" rule is productive pressure
Requiring participants to pull from each card category forced unexpected combinations. Fruzsina's brain-wave virus emerged from connecting biology (Restorative Practices) with AI resistance (Slavery) in ways neither concept would have suggested alone.
05
Facilitation
Speculative permission must be explicitly granted
When asked "does it need to be feasible?", Armin's answer — "No, that's not the point — it's called speculative design" — visibly unlocked more ambitious thinking. Without that explicit permission, participants defaulted to practical solutions. The framing matters as much as the cards.
06
Card Design
Provocative trigger cards (Slavery) work — use them carefully
The Slavery card produced an immediate visceral reaction, opening conversations about AI labour exploitation that wouldn't have surfaced otherwise. Provocative framing is a feature, not a bug — but it requires the facilitator to hold the context clearly and redirect if needed.
07
Process
The game produces baselines, not final answers
As Armin noted: "we come up with these ideas along these activities and it might not be the end product we prototype, but a baseline — from which you can start to reverse engineer: which part can work in the present?" The game's value is in opening the design space, not closing it.
08
Card Design
Making the cards is itself the learning
"Since we've been having this course, I watch the world more consciously — you easily pick up things. Things are happening, but you start to realise things that are not." The act of observing and curating weak signals changes how you perceive your own context.

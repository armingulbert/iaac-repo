Coming together
is the hardest part.

A personal learning journey through the fabrication of the AI Chapel — five rounds of prototyping, one retro screen, and everything that happens between a concept and an experience.

AI ChapelFabricationDesign Studio 2
The real challenge

I thought the hardest part would be cutting wood. It wasn’t. Wood is patient. It does what you ask. The real challenge was much harder to name: making everything — the structure, the light, the sound, the screen, the silence — arrive at the same place at the same time and feel like one thing.

A doctor doesn’t diagnose in the corridor. A lawyer doesn’t think through a case in the elevator. They go somewhere quiet. Intentional. They close the door. That is what the AI Chapel had to be: not a prototype, but a place. Getting there required five rounds of building before the final installation.

“The risk was never the technology. The risk was never stopping to ask what using it was doing to us.”

Early rough proto set up in the dark room — first sense of atmosphere

Building physical needs a plan

Designing on screen and building in space are different disciplines. I ordered wood before the structure design was finalised. Some of it didn’t fit as the idea evolved. Time lost. Lesson absorbed.

But a plan is only as good as your understanding of the material — and that understanding only comes from making. By round three, I was adjusting the workplan in real time. Not because the plan was wrong, but because the material taught me things no drawing could anticipate: how the panels wanted to sit, where the joints needed reinforcement, what the CRT screen’s weight actually meant for the altar structure.

Audiovisual work is slower than you think

Testing projection, building the TouchDesigner data capture layer, finding the right light, fine-tuning the atmosphere — every one of these took longer than planned. Not because anything went wrong, but because AV installations are systems, and systems need time to find their equilibrium.

Material selection matters more than expected in this context. The black panels that form the chapel walls aren’t just structural — they define the quality of the light inside the space. The wrong surface would have broken the atmosphere entirely. Every material in an AV installation is also a design decision.

“If light, sound, and space need to feel like one thing, budget twice the time you think you need for that final integration phase.”

Light, altar, and screen finding their equilibrium — the chapel as atmosphere

What five rounds taught me
01  ·  Plan

Building physical requires a structural plan before touching material — not a sketch, a real assembly logic.

02  ·  Adapt

Adjust the workplan as the material teaches you. Iteration isn’t failure — it’s the only honest path to understanding.

03  ·  Prototype

Five rounds before the final. Each one taught something the previous could not have anticipated.

04  ·  Interaction

Interaction design is not a layer you add at the end. It shapes the physical from the beginning.

05  ·  AV Time

Audiovisual installations need double the time for integration. Systems need time to find their equilibrium.

06  ·  Materials

Every material in an AV installation is a design decision. The wrong surface breaks the atmosphere entirely.

07  ·  Environment

A neat, inventoried setup is not pedantry — it is the difference between the work you designed and the work the audience experiences.

And then the speaker control went missing on installation day. Twenty percent below intended volume. Not catastrophic — but enough to know it. The sound of the chapel was part of its argument, and that argument arrived slightly diminished. Installation day is not the time to discover what is missing.

In closing

The chapel is standing. It works. And I understand it now in a way I could not have understood it from a screen — because I built it five times first.

The hardest part was never the wood. It was making the structure, the light, the sound, the screen, and the silence arrive at the same place, at the same time, and feel like one thing.

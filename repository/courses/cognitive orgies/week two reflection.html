SAFE SPACE — A Home Office Intervention.
A week reflection on building a voice-to-experience pipeline in TouchDesigner,
and what it taught me about listening, thresholds, and the spaces we speak into existence.
01Context

My research has been circling one question: can AI create the presence of a space that physically does not exist? Not simulate it on a screen — actually make you feel inside it, surrounded by it, authored by it.

SAFE SPACE was the first serious attempt. A handmade fabric canopy in a home office. You enter, speak your ideal workspace aloud, and the walls around you change to reflect what you described. Voice as architectural tool. Language as spatial material.

You speak a room into existence.
The room appears around you,
softly, on fabric.

But this sits inside a deliberate contradiction. The same system that creates your imagined space records everything you say, processes it through corporate AI infrastructure, and logs it to disk. The canopy offers refuge and enacts surveillance simultaneously. That tension is not a bug in the concept — it is the concept. I want to keep building inside that contradiction next term, around the future of work, consent, and what we silently trade for comfort.

02The moment
it worked
TD network — live generation
Canopy interior — projection on fabric

After two days of empty WAV files, misfiring callbacks, and recordings that captured nothing but room noise — the TouchDesigner TextPort printed this:

// take_20260305_163617 — sending to OpenAI...
WHISPER: file too small - no speech detected
WHISPER: cooldown 1.3s remaining - ignoring
WHISPER: cooldown 0.7s remaining - ignoring
WHISPER: result -> Hello?
WHISPER: transcript_output updated
"fuck this is crazy" — first reaction. then: yes, solved.

Both were true at once. There was something genuinely strange about seeing a human voice become text in a node — a moment where the pipeline stopped being infrastructure and briefly felt like something that listened. Then the technical relief. Then back to work. That double reaction is worth sitting with. The moment the system works is also the moment it stops being transparent.

04What the
microphone
taught me

Building a voice-triggered pipeline turned out to be a lesson in thresholds — not just technically but conceptually. The system had to learn to distinguish intentional speech from ambient noise. The failures were instructive.

Learning 01
Sensing is editorial
The RMS threshold is not a technical parameter — it is a design decision. Set it too low and the room speaks. Too high and the person disappears. Where you draw that line determines whose voice the system hears.
Learning 02
Speech dynamics are uneven
People do not speak at a consistent volume. A sentence starts loud and trails quiet. A question rises. Silence mid-thought is not the end of speaking. A fixed threshold misses all of this.
Learning 03
Intent ≠ interpretation
What the participant described, what Whisper transcribed, and what the image model generated were three different things. That gap is not a failure — it is the most interesting material in the system. The space that appears is a negotiation, not a reflection.
Next step
The system speaks back
Voice-over as a reflection layer — text-to-speech responding to what was heard. The experience becomes a dialogue. The space starts to have a voice of its own.
05The contradiction
I am keeping

The project critiques surveillance while using surveillance infrastructure. OpenAI's servers processed every word spoken inside the canopy. The microphone never stopped listening. I built this on purpose. Using the tools of the thing you are critiquing is not hypocrisy — it is the honest position. There is no outside from which to make this work cleanly.

The offer
A space that hears you and builds your world
↔
The cost
Every word logged, processed, stored

The final question in the experience — "if your employer paid for your apartment, would you trade your privacy for financial security?" — is not rhetorical. People are already making that trade. Next term I want to go further: specific policies, real data practices, named companies. The more precise the critique, the stranger and more affecting the experience.
